Lecture

 
 

Module 4 introduced critical thinking. Essentially, the aims of critical thinking are:

  • To pinpoint and minimize biasing influences from culture and upbringing.
  • To seek out and be guided by knowledge and evidence that fits with reality.
  • To think rather than believe.
  • To be willing to change beliefs when evidence demands it.

The following video clips (YouTube) present the following concepts:

LOGIC

Logic is a way to combine ideas to come to a conclusion.

Logic helps us combine established ideas with a new idea.

Logic is a way to decide whether you should agree or wait for more information.

BROKEN LOGIC

Premise #1 + premise#2 = conclusion

Logical fallacy

Breaking the rules of logic can make an answer seem right when it isn’t.

STRAW MEN

Example - Vaccinations

Oversimplifying a disagreement to black and white, for and against, true or false. This can be misleading.

When the arguments for and against become increasingly extreme, they are said to be polarised.

There can be more than one solution to a problem.

GETTING PERSONAL (PLAY THE BALL, NOT THE PLAYER)

I am not my opinions.

Linking dislike with disbelief is dangerous.

Experts – good for advice, but just because an ‘expert’ says it, doesn’t make it true.

An ‘expert’ can be found to say just about anything!

THE GAMBLER'S FALLACY

Patterns - our brain can see patterns even when they aren’t there.

The assumption that probability changes depending on past results is a fallacy.

Logic plus probability = link? Or Coincidence?

Occurrence needs to be tested again and again.

THE PRECAUTIONARY PRINCIPLE

Not acting until you have a good idea of any adverse consequences is called The Precautionary Principle.

In science, a theory is a well-tested rule which is based on logic, explains repeated observation and has been used to make accurate predictions.

100% certainty is not possible and we know that this doesn’t worry critical thinkers!
